Elks.partial = xtabs(count~Action+Vehicle+Traffic, Elks.table)

LOW TRAFFIC

We perform Pearsons chi-squared test.A statistical test applied to sets of categorical data to evaluate how likely it is that any observed difference between the sets arose by chance.

Elks.low.Traffic = Elks.partial[,,1]
chisq.test(Elks.low.Traffic)
## 
##  Pearson's Chi-squared test with Yates' continuity correction
## 
## data:  Elks.low.Traffic
## X-squared = 42.644, df = 1, p-value = 6.566e-11

Since the p-value is very little we refuse the null hypothesis. In low traffic conditions the action made by the elk is related to the kind of vehicle is passing.

We draw a mosaic plot

mosaic(t(Elks.low.Traffic), gp=shading_max, split_vertical=TRUE, labeling = labeling_values, main = "low traffic")

The mosaic plot confirm the result of Pearsons chi-squared test and add other informations: in low traffic conditions elks are prone to ro retreat if there is a truck and to cross if the vehicle is a car.

We calculate the odds ratio.

or.low = oddsratio(Elks.low.Traffic, log=FALSE)
or.low
##  odds ratios for Action and Vehicle 
## 
## [1] 5.286842

or.low ~= 5.29>>1 this means the probability to get a an elk cross the road is much higher than if there was a truck.

Confidence interval

confint(or.low)
##                               2.5 %   97.5 %
## Crossing:Retreat/Car:Truck 3.149567 8.874459

The odds ratio is surely bigger than one

comparison between HIGH and LOW traffic

We plot a fourfold plot for both low and high traffic. Following suitable standardization, the cell frequencies of a 2x2 table are shown as a quarter circle whose radius is proportional to frequencys square root, so that its area is proportional to the cell frequency. An association (odds ratio different from 1) between the binary row and column variables is indicated by the tendency of diagonally opposite cells in one direction to differ in size from those in the other direction; color is used to show this direction. Confidence rings for the odds ratio allow a visual test of the null of no association; the rings for adjacent quadrants overlap iff the observed counts are consistent with the null hypothesis.

fourfold(Elks.partial)

From low traffic fourfold plot we can see the odd rate is very different from one since the diagonally opposed cells have very different dimensions. Also the confidence rings for odds ratio dont overlap.Action and vehicle are not indipendent. In high traffic plot we can see the opposite: all cells dimensions are almost the same and the confidence rings overlaps almost perfectly. We can conclude when the traffic is high action ad vehicle are indipendent.

We plot on the same plot the two odds ratios and their confidence intervals.

plot(or.part)

Surely low traffics odds ratio is bigger than high traffics one.

HOMOGENUES ASSOCIATION

We perform Woolf test on homogeneous association.

woolf_test(Elks.partial)
## 
##  Woolf-test on Homogeneity of Odds Ratios (no 3-Way assoc.)
## 
## data:  Elks.partial
## X-squared = 14.999, df = 1, p-value = 0.0001076

The p- value is very little, so we refuse the null hypothesisi which states that the log odds ratio are the same through the two different contingency tables. This means it is impossible to extract from these data a rule that generalizes well for both the tables. It is better to analyze them separately low and high traffic.
